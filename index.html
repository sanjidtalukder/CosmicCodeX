GLOBAL LAND-OCEAN TEMPERATURE INDEX

Key point:
Earth was about 2.45 degrees Fahrenheit (or about 1.36 degrees Celsius)
warmer in 2023 than in the late 19th-century (1850-1900) preindustrial average.
The 10 most recent years are the warmest on record.
The combined land and ocean temperature has increased at an average rate of 0.11°F (0.06°C) per decade since 1850.
However, since 1982, the rate of warming has accelerated to 0.36°F (0.20°C) per decade.

GLOBAL ICE SHEETS

Key point:
Antarctica and Greenland are home to massive ice sheets. These ice sheets store about two-thirds of all the fresh water on Earth.
That’s an incredible amount!
Unfortunately, both Antarctica and Greenland have been losing ice mass since 2002.
Here are the numbers: Antarctica: It’s losing ice at an average rate of about 150 billion tons per year .
Greenland: It’s losing about 270 billion tons per year .

GLOBAL SEA LEVEL

Key point:
Global sea levels are rising as a result of human-caused global warming,
with recent rates being unprecedented over the past 2,500-plus years.
The annual rate of sea level rise has increased significantly:
In 1993, it was about 0.08 inches/year (0.20 centimeters/year).
Currently, the yearly rate is approximately 0.17 inches/year (0.44 centimeters/year).

GLOBAL LAND-OCEAN CARBON DIOXIDE & METHANE

Key point(Carbon Dioxide):
Carbon dioxide in the atmosphere warms the planet, causing climate change.
Human activities have raised the atmosphere’s carbon dioxide content by 50% in less than 200 years.
If carbon dioxide were removed, the terrestrial greenhouse effect would collapse, and Earth’s surface temperature would drop significantly, by approximately 33°C (59°F).

Key point(Methan):
Methane is a powerful heat-trapping gas.
An estimated 60% of today’s methane emissions are the result of human activities.
A molecule of methane traps more heat than a molecule of CO2, but methane has a relatively short lifespan of 7 to 12 years in the atmosphere,
while CO2 can persist for hundreds of years or more.
